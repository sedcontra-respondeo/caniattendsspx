QUAESTIO DISPUTATA

Concerning the Society of Saint Pius X:
Whether Its Clergy Are Suspended, Excommunicated, or Schismatic.
And Whether it is Permitted to Receive the Sacraments From Them.


This document is dedicated to the Blessed Virgin Mary.
It is written by Traditional Priests who wish to remain anonymous.

The views expressed in this work represent the personal theological and canonical analysis of the authors.
All citations are to primary sources; readers are invited to verify every reference independently.

Scope and Limit of This Analysis

The canonical and theological questions examined in this work concern exclusively the Society of Saint Pius X and its clergy (SSPX or the Society). Nothing argued here applies to communities of the Roman Rite in full hierarchical communion with the Apostolic See, including the Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter, the Institute of Christ the King Sovereign Priest, the Institute of the Good Shepherd, and analogous institutes whose status was established or confirmed by competent ecclesiastical authority. These communities differ from the Society not merely in degree but in kind: They have accepted the jurisdiction of the Roman Pontiff not only in principle but in the concrete institutional form that acceptance requires, precisely the act the Society’s founders declined in 1988. The wound this work examines is specific; its diagnosis is not a general pathology of attachment to the traditional liturgy, and it must not be read as one.

A Note on Canonical Citations

Throughout this work, the penal canons of the 1983 Code of Canon Law are cited by their current numbering, with the pre-2021 numbering supplied parenthetically. The revision of Book VI promulgated by the apostolic constitution Pascite Gregem Dei, which entered into force on December 8, 2021, renumbered several of the penal canons bearing on this analysis. For example, the delict of consecrating a bishop without a pontifical mandate, formerly Canon 1382, is now Canon 1387, and the latae sententiae suspension incurred by the one ordained without lawful dimissorial letters, formerly Canon 1383, is now Canon 1388. Canon 1364, treating schism, was not renumbered and is therefore cited without a parenthetical. Accordingly, a citation in the form “Canon 1387 (1382)” refers to a single canon under both its current and its former designation in order to aid the reader with an older edition of Canon Law.
