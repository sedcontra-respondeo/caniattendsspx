Conclusion

Subject to the premises established in Article 1 — that SSPX priests are suspended by canonical act and papal declaration, and that the Church therefore does not tolerate them in the ministry in the sense Aquinas requires — the faithful who knowingly receive Holy Communion from SSPX priests commit sin. They participate in the sin of the ministers whom the Church no longer tolerates to act in her name. See Summa Theologiae III, Q.64, A.6, Reply to Objection 2.1 Membership in the Catholic Church requires the three bonds of unity:2 same Sacraments, same Faith, and same Hierarchy. Habitual participation in the sacramental life of a body operating outside legitimate hierarchical authority endangers the third bond in ways the faithful should not lightly risk.

Saint Thomas marks the further consequence for the fruit of such a Mass: the severed priest still confects the sacrament validly, yet the prayers he offers in the Church's name are without efficacy, Summa Theologiae III, Q.82, A.7, Reply to Objection 3:

'The priest, in reciting the prayers of the Mass, speaks instead of the Church, in whose unity he remains; but in consecrating the sacrament he speaks as in the person of Christ, Whose place he holds by the power of his orders. Consequently, if a priest severed from the unity of the Church celebrates Mass, not having lost the power of order, he consecrates Christ's true body and blood; but because he is severed from the unity of the Church, his prayers have no efficacy.'

It is important to state clearly what this conclusion does and does not claim. It does not claim that a Vatican document explicitly declares such reception a grave sin — no such document has been located. The conclusion is derived from applying Aquinas's principle to the canonical situation established in Article 1. The strength of the conclusion therefore depends on the suspension argument established in Article 1: that SSPX priests are suspended by canonical act and papal declaration, and that this constitutes non-toleration in the ministerial sense Aquinas requires. Those who hold that the SSPX necessity argument successfully defeats the suspension — and the Society's canonists have pressed it (see Article 1, Objection 4, and Article 3, Objection 3) — will not be moved by the present conclusion. The purpose of this document is not to resolve that underlying canonical dispute but to demonstrate that it has serious moral consequences that cannot be set aside.

The consecration of four new SSPX bishops on July 1, 2026 — carried out despite the Holy See’s explicit warning that it would be a schismatic act3 carrying automatic excommunication4, and despite Pope Leo XIV’s personal appeal of June 29 — has deepened rather than resolved the canonical irregularity on which this conclusion rests. On July 2, 2026, the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith declared that the consecrating and consecrated bishops had incurred latae sententiae excommunication, and that the Society’s clergy and formally adhering faithful are in schism. This second generation of illicit episcopal consecrations mirrors 1988 in every essential respect5 and forecloses the argument that the SSPX situation is trending toward normalization. Nor are the episcopal norms violated here a novelty of the postconciliar Church — they predate the Second Vatican Council by centuries: from the fourth canon of Nicaea through Trent, from Pius VI's Charitas through the Code of Pope Saint Pius X to the present law, the requirement that no man be made a bishop against the will of the Church's supreme authority spans the whole of the tradition the Society claims to defend (see Article 2, Reply to Objection 4).

A further observation emerges from the FSSP's founding theological essay.6 The SSPX's stated position is that the Pope is legitimate, ordinary obedience is required, but extraordinary necessity justifies resistance in the present crisis. This position deserves to be stated in its strongest form before being examined. The SSPX does not claim to be sedevacantist. It claims to recognize papal authority in principle while acting outside its exercise in practice, on grounds of necessity. This is a coherent statement of intent. The question is whether it is coherent in execution. The FSSP authors identify the tension: if the reigning Pope possesses full and legitimate authority — which the SSPX affirms — then his explicit, personal, repeated prohibition of the 1988 consecrations was a legitimate exercise of that authority. To act against it on grounds of 'necessity' while affirming his legitimacy requires showing that the necessity was genuine and that no legitimate means were available. The Protocol of Accord of 1988, signed by Lefebvre and repudiated the following morning, shows legitimate means were available and refused. The July 1, 2026 consecrations, proceeding after Leo XIV's personal appeal and Rome's formal offer of dialogue, repeat this pattern. At each point where the necessity argument might have been validated by the absence of alternatives, an alternative was offered and declined. The SSPX's position is not incoherent in principle; it is defeated in fact by the historical record of refused solutions.

A note on the language of this document. Some will observe that the Catholic Church today generally avoids the words schism and schismatic in its official dealings with separated communities. The Orthodox are not called schismatics in modern Vatican documents. Protestants are not called heretics. The Second Vatican Council's own Decree on Ecumenism adopted the language of separated brethren for these communities, and that gentler vocabulary has shaped official usage since.7 The SSPX invokes this modern pastoral restraint to argue that applying schism language to them is anachronistic, aggressive, or indicative of a pre-conciliar mentality.

This argument, while accurately describing modern practice, actually cuts against the SSPX rather than for it. The Council's own decree on ecumenism supplies the reason for the gentler vocabulary, and that reason is fatal to the Society's use of it: 'The children who are born into these Communities and who grow up believing in Christ cannot be accused of the sin involved in the separation' (Unitatis Redintegratio 3).8 The softened language reflects the Church's recognition that those born and raised in separated communities did not personally commit the act of separation — they cannot be charged with willfully breaking communion. The SSPX has no such defense. Archbishop Lefebvre was a bishop of the Catholic Church in full communion, who was personally warned, who signed and repudiated an accord, and who proceeded against explicit papal prohibition. This is not the inherited situation of an Orthodox Christian or a cradle Protestant. It is a fresh act of separation by someone who knew exactly what he was doing and was told explicitly by the Pope what the consequences would be.

Furthermore, the language of schism and schismatic act was not imposed on the SSPX by traditionalist critics speaking anachronistically. It was applied to them in 1988 by the Pope himself in real time. Pope John Paul II used the precise word schismatic in Ecclesia Dei adflicta on July 2, 1988 — two days after the consecrations. Cardinal Gantin's decree of excommunication cited Canon 1364,9 the canon for schism, alongside Canon 1387 (1382). The SSPX was thus not called schismatic by general retrospective labeling: they were called schismatic by the formal canonical act that created their situation, by the Pope whose legitimacy they simultaneously affirm. Leo XIV's Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith used identical language in May 2026. The word has been consistent throughout. What has changed is not the Church's canonical assessment but the SSPX's hope that modern pastoral language might obscure it.

The reactivation of this language in 2026 is not a reversion to pre-conciliar harshness. It is the application of the traditional doctrine of medicinal penalty to a situation that nearly four decades of accompaniment and dialogue have not resolved.10 Canon law is explicit: censures including excommunication are medicinal in intent — imposed not to punish vindictively but to bring the offender to repentance and restoration of communion. To accompany indefinitely without correction is not mercy — it is the ratification of a canonical situation the Church has consistently identified as harmful to those within it and to the unity of the Body of Christ. The purpose of calling the act what it is — schismatic — and applying the penalty the law provides, is precisely the restoration of those who have departed, not their condemnation.

It is worth noting that the SSPX argues the faithful should avoid Novus Ordo Masses on the basis of possible theological deficiency and probable spiritual danger — a standard based on possibility and inference. Those who accept this probabilistic standard have all the more reason to take seriously the present argument, which rests on documented canonical fact rather than probability.11
