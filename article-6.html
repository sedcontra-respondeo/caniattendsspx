Article 6

Whether the Faithful Sin by Merely Attending the Mass of the SSPX Without Receiving Holy Communion.

Objection 1. St. Thomas Speaks of Receiving a Sacrament, Not Mere Attendance

Saint Thomas's principle at Q.64 A.61 specifies that one who receives a sacrament from a suspended minister sins. Mere physical attendance at Mass without sacramental reception does not constitute receiving a sacrament from the minister. Thomas's language does not extend to presence without reception. Therefore mere attendance without reception does not fall under Thomas's condemnation.

Return to top

Objection 2. Occasional Attendance Is Distinct from Formal Adherence

Furthermore the 1996 PCILT clarification2 explicitly distinguished occasional attendance without the schismatic spirit from formal adherence to schism. If Rome itself draws this distinction, occasional attendance without reception cannot constitute the kind of sin that would require avoidance altogether.

Return to top

Objection 3. Ecclesia Dei Permitted Fulfillment of Your Sunday Obligation

Furthermore, the Holy See itself has repeatedly affirmed that the faithful may fulfill the Sunday obligation at an SSPX Mass. Msgr. Camille Perl, Secretary of the Pontifical Commission Ecclesia Dei, wrote in 1996, and repeated in 1998, that "in the strict sense you may fulfill your Sunday obligation by attending a Mass celebrated by a priest of the Society of Saint Pius X,"3 and the Commission stated publicly in 2003 that one who attends simply to participate in a Mass according to the 1962 Missal for the sake of devotion does not sin, adding that a modest contribution to the collection could be justified.4 But an act by which a grave precept of the Church is satisfied cannot at the same time be a sin against her. Therefore attendance without reception is licit.

Return to top

On the contrary, (DDF Explicitly Tells Faithful to Abstain From Attendance, Not Just Reception of Holy Communion)

The Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith, in its Explanatory Note of 2 July 2026, exhorts all the faithful "to abstain from participating in the celebrations and activities promoted by the aforementioned Priestly Fraternity of Saint Pius X."5 The Apostolic See here addresses not the reception of the sacraments but participation itself.

I answer that

Saint Thomas states the governing principle, Summa Theologiae III, Q.82, A.9:

'Heretical, schismatical, excommunicate, or even sinful priests, although they have the power to consecrate the Eucharist, yet they do not make a proper use of it; on the contrary, they sin by using it. But whoever communicates with another who is in sin, becomes a sharer in his sin. Consequently, it is not lawful to receive Communion from them, or to assist at their Mass. Still there is a difference among the above, because heretics, schismatics, and excommunicates, have been forbidden, by the Church's sentence, to perform the Eucharistic rite. And therefore whoever hears their Mass or receives the sacraments from them, commits sin.'

Mere attendance at an SSPX Mass without reception of Holy Communion occupies a genuinely grey area — one that intellectual honesty requires acknowledging directly. Saint Thomas's precise language — 'receives a sacrament from him' — does not obviously extend to mere physical presence without sacramental reception. This objection is valid and is not fully resolved by the primary argument.

However four practical qualifications are necessary. First, the theoretical grey area does not describe the actual situation of most of the faithful addressed. Whatever the older discipline of infrequent communion, the faithful who make an SSPX chapel their regular provision today do not, as a class, permanently abstain from its communion rail. In practice, habitual SSPX attendance means habitual reception from suspended ministers. Second, even where reception is absent, habitual exclusive attendance at SSPX structures — combined with use of their confessional, their marriage tribunal,6 their schools, and their publications — constitutes evidence of formal adherence under the criteria identified by the PCILT, though formal adherence in the canonical sense also requires the internal element of agreement with the substance of the schism.7 Third, if reception of Holy Communion from these ministers is gravely illicit — as the preceding Article concludes — then habitual attendance at their Masses constitutes a voluntary proximate occasion of that sin. The moral theologians teach that one who freely and without proportionate cause places himself in the proximate occasion of grave sin thereby sins gravely by that very fact.8 Of the classical categories of occasion — persons, places, and things — all three converge in the weekly frequentation of an SSPX chapel: the minister who will offer Communion, the place ordered to its distribution, and the sacred action that invites it. Nor is presence the only cooperation involved: attendance swells the congregation whose numbers the Society cites in its own vindication, and contribution to the collection materially sustains a ministry now under declared censure. Fourth, while in ordinary doubts of law the faithful may follow a solidly probable opinion, the received moral theology binds them to the safer course where the danger of grave sin is proximate and voluntary — and that, by the third point above, is precisely the case here. In dubio, tutior pars eligenda applies not as a general rule of conduct but in its uncontested zone: where what is risked is not inconvenience but grave sin itself9.

Reply to Objection 1. Thomas's Language Is Granted — but the Voluntary Occasion of Sin Remains

This objection is granted as far as it goes. Thomas's language does not clearly condemn mere attendance without reception, and the grey area is genuinely acknowledged. That concession, however, is owed only to the passage the objection cites; Saint Thomas speaks to attendance in its own right, Summa Theologiae III, Q.82, A.9:

'Heretical, schismatical, excommunicate, or even sinful priests, although they have the power to consecrate the Eucharist, yet they do not make a proper use of it; on the contrary, they sin by using it. But whoever communicates with another who is in sin, becomes a sharer in his sin. Consequently, it is not lawful to receive Communion from them, or to assist at their Mass. Still there is a difference among the above, because heretics, schismatics, and excommunicates, have been forbidden, by the Church's sentence, to perform the Eucharistic rite. And therefore whoever hears their Mass or receives the sacraments from them, commits sin.'

His words reach the hearing of their Mass, not the reception of a sacrament alone. The practical argument above — that habitual attendance rarely exists without reception — addresses the pastoral situation without resolving the theoretical question. What escapes the grey area entirely, however, is the question of occasion: one who grants that reception is gravely illicit cannot treat weekly presence at its offering as morally neutral, for the reasons given in the body of the Article.

Reply to Objection 2. PCILT Distinction Regards Schism Specifically and Does Not Answer Moral Liceity of Attendance

The PCILT distinction between occasional attendance and formal adherence10 is correctly cited and its weight acknowledged. That distinction was made in the context of the canonical crime of schism, not the full moral question attending SSPX participation. It establishes a floor below which canonical schism is not incurred — not a ceiling above which no moral concern arises. Moreover, the faithful who attend habitually are surrounded by those who formally adhere and are formed week by week by preaching that defends the Society's positions; in such an environment, resistance to formal adherence grows steadily more difficult. What was said in the body of the Article concerning the voluntary occasion of sin applies to this danger as well.

Reply to Objection 3. Toleration Has Been Formally Withdrawn — Canonical Sufficiency Was Never Moral Permission

This objection states the strongest case for attendance, and its sources are genuine; yet its arguments do not hold. Three things must be said.

First, every one of these responses belonged to a posture of toleration that has now been formally terminated. The Ecclesia Dei Commission was created precisely to accompany the faithful attached to the Society back toward full communion, and its letters were issued while Rome assumed a more lenient posture toward the Society's ministers, in the hope that they would respond in kind and reconcile. That toleration, moreover, was always directed at the faithful rather than the ministers: the Commission's responses addressed whether attendance was sinful for the one attending, while the priests themselves remained suspended throughout, without faculties, their ministry never approved. Saint Thomas's principle hinges on this: one may receive from — a fortiori assist at the Mass of — a minister whom the Church tolerates in the ministry. The Decree of 2 July 2026 removes the hinge. The same Apostolic See that once wrote that it could not recommend attendance now exhorts the faithful to abstain from participation altogether. A dicasterial courtesy of 2003 cannot survive the contrary dicasterial acts of 2026; the later and more authoritative acts govern.

Second, the objection collapses canonical sufficiency into moral permission. Even granting — as this document itself argues — that the Society's ministers remain Catholics under the Church's internal discipline, so that their Masses may satisfy the letter of Canon 1248 §1,11 the question whether an act satisfies the precept is distinct from the question whether the act is licit. The precept obliges assistance at Mass; it does not permit assistance at any Mass regardless of circumstances or canonical situation. The Commission's own 2003 letter12 embodies the distinction the objection ignores: in the same document it stated that while the obligation could "in the strict sense" be fulfilled, it nonetheless could not recommend attendance, and warned that attendance with a schismatic intention is sinful. What was already a discouraged sufficiency under toleration cannot become a positive permission after toleration has been withdrawn.

Third, the same reasoning governs the collection. What was a justifiable modest contribution toward tolerated ministers is, after 2 July 2026, material support of a ministry the Apostolic See has directed the faithful to avoid — a cooperation treated in the body of this Article.

Thomae Aquinatis, tomus III, 12th ed., rev. Engelbertus M. Münch, O.P. (Friburgi Brisgoviae: Herder, 1955), no. 451 (Regula 3): Deneganda est absolutio poenitenti, qui recusat deserere occasionem proximam voluntariam graviter peccandi. Ratio est, quia ille, qui voluntarie in occasione proxima vult remanere, amat periculum peccandi, ac proinde non habet neque veram contritionem de peccatis praeteritis neque verum propositum de peccatis futuris vitandis. Hinc damnavit Innocentius XI d. 2 Martii 1679 propositionem 61: ‘Potest aliquando absolvi, qui in proxima occasione peccandi versatur, quam potest et non vult omittere, quin immo directe et ex proposito quaerit, aut ei se ingerit’; prop. 62: ‘Proxima occasio peccandi non est fugienda, quando causa aliqua utili aut honesta non fugiendi occurrit’; prop. 63: ‘Licitum est quaerere directe occasionem proximam peccandi pro bono spirituali vel temporali nostro vel proximi.’ In translation: “Rule 3. Absolution must be denied to a penitent who refuses to abandon a voluntary proximate occasion of mortal sin. The reason is that one who wishes to remain voluntarily in a proximate occasion loves the danger of sinning, and therefore has neither true contrition for past sins nor a true purpose of avoiding future sins. Hence Innocent XI, on 2 March 1679, condemned proposition 61: ‘A man can sometimes be absolved who is found in a proximate occasion of sinning, which he is able to and does not wish to give up, but rather directly and deliberately seeks or brings himself into it’; proposition 62: ‘A proximate occasion of sin does not need to be avoided when some useful or honorable reason for not avoiding it presents itself’; proposition 63: ‘It is lawful to seek directly a proximate occasion of sin for the spiritual good or temporal good either for ourselves or that of our neighbor.’”↩︎
