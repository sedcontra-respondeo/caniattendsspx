Article 5

Whether the Faithful Commit Sin by Receiving Holy Communion from the SSPX.

Objection 1. Because a Priest Says Mass in the Name of the Church, Faithful Do Not Sin by Receiving From a Sinful Priest.

A sinful priest in good canonical standing celebrates Mass on behalf of the Church. His personal sin does not communicate to the faithful because they receive Communion not from him personally but from the Church whose minister he is. The same sacramental act occurs at an SSPX Mass — valid matter, valid form, valid orders. The faithful therefore receive from Christ through a validly ordained priest and do not participate in any irregularity.

Objection 2. Hawaii Five and Msgr. Perl Decisions Say This Is Permitted

Furthermore, Cardinal Ratzinger ruled in the case of the Hawaii Five that attendance at an SSPX chapel does not constitute the canonical crime of schism.1 And in 2003 Msgr. Camille Perl of the Pontifical Commission Ecclesia Dei wrote that the faithful can fulfill their Sunday obligation at an SSPX Mass and that a modest contribution to the collection is not sinful.2 If the future Pope Benedict XVI ruled that attendance is canonically insufficient for schism, it cannot constitute mortal sin to receive Communion at such a Mass. Canon law and moral theology ought to be consistent.

See ReplyReturn to top

Objection 3. Due to Necessity, Supplied Jurisdiction, and/or epikeia Attendance Is Licit

Furthermore, the Society’s canonical defense rests on theologians and canonists of high standing — Prümmer, Roberti, Palazzini — whose principles of supplied jurisdiction it applies to its own case, and contemporary commentators in full communion have endorsed the application: SSPX sacraments, on this reasoning, are licitly attended on the basis of supplied jurisdiction, epikeia, or necessity. Where an application of received principles is defended by serious authors, a probable opinion exists in moral theology.3 Acting on a probable opinion is generally licit.

See ReplyReturn to top

Objection 4. Pope Francis’ Faculties for Confessions and Marriage Prove Church Tolerates SSPX Sacraments

Furthermore, Pope Francis's permanent grant of supplied jurisdiction for absolution in SSPX confessionals suggests Rome does not regard SSPX sacramental ministry as something the faithful must categorically avoid. If Rome wanted the faithful to abstain entirely from SSPX sacraments, it would not have institutionalized access to them.4

Objection 5. If the Church Permits Reception from Eastern Orthodox, She A Fortiori Permits Sacraments From the SSPX

The Church does not categorically forbid communicatio in sacris with those outside full communion. Canon 844 §25 explicitly permits the faithful to receive penance, the Eucharist, and anointing of the sick from non-Catholic ministers whose churches possess valid sacraments — precisely the situation of Eastern Orthodox clergy — whenever necessity or genuine spiritual advantage commends it, provided danger of error or indifferentism is avoided and approach to a Catholic minister is physically or morally impossible. This was applied concretely to the faithful behind the Iron Curtain and in regions such as Siberia, where Catholic clergy were inaccessible for extended periods. The principle establishes that access to valid sacraments can take precedence over strict ecclesial boundaries.

If this is true of the Orthodox — a body in formal, centuries-old schism that does not even nominally recognize the Roman primacy — it applies with greater force to the SSPX. SSPX priests are not ministers of a separated church; they are validly ordained Catholic priests who profess the whole of Catholic doctrine, including the papal primacy in principle, differing from clergy in good standing only by a canonical irregularity that is itself disputed (Article 1). If the faithful may licitly receive from an Orthodox priest who denies the dogma of the papacy outright, it is incongruous to forbid reception from an SSPX priest who affirms it.

Further, ‘moral impossibility’ should be read pastorally, not narrowly. For many traditionally-minded Catholics, the only Mass reasonably accessible is the Novus Ordo, which they hold — on grounds shared by clergy in good standing — to be liturgically and doctrinally compromised. Moral impossibility has long included grave inconvenience, not only physical distance; being unable in good conscience to participate in a liturgy one holds gravely deficient is arguably such a hardship. On this reading, the faithful with only the Novus Ordo available are not unlike the faithful behind the Iron Curtain: a Catholic sacrament is nominally present, but burdensome enough to justify seeking it elsewhere.

Objection 6. Canon 1335 Lifts Censures When Faithful Legitimately Request the Sacraments

Furthermore, the Code itself resolves the question in favor of the faithful. Canon 1335 §2 (1335) provides that if a censure prohibits the celebration of sacraments or sacramentals, the prohibition is suspended whenever this is necessary to care for the faithful in danger of death; and that if a latae sententiae censure has not been declared, the prohibition is moreover suspended whenever a member of the faithful requests a sacrament, a sacramental, or an act of governance — which request the faithful may make for any just cause.6 Whatever the force of the argument from suspension (Article 1), no decree of suspension names the individual SSPX priest specifically; the priest ordained at Écône in 2015 or 2020 incurred his censure, if at all, latae sententiae and undeclared. The Decree of July 2, 2026 confirms this on its face: it declares censures for six bishops alone, and warns clerics and lay faithful only in the conditional — that they would incur excommunication by adhering to the schism — thereby acknowledging that they have not yet incurred it.7 Penal laws are of strict interpretation (Canon 18), and the accompanying Nota Esplicativa's present-tense assertion that the Society's ministers are in schism cannot enlarge what the Decree, the only instrument in penal form, actually declares. Nor can the 1996 Note supply the defect: it is not an authentic interpretation under Canon 16 §1, never having received specific pontifical approval, but an opinion of the Pontifical Council published in Communicationes.8 When the faithful approach him and request Communion, the law itself lifts his prohibition for the duration of that act. The Church, through this canon, tolerates the minister in precisely the moment of ministry at issue. Aquinas's principle therefore does not apply: the faithful who request a sacrament using this canon do not communicate in the sin of a minister whom the Church Herself, through this canon, explicitly permits to minister to the one requesting. The devout desire for the Traditional Mass and sacraments is manifestly a just cause, and nothing in the canon confines it to a single occasion.

On the contrary, (A Man Receiving From a Non-Tolerated Minister Communicates in His Sin)

St. Thomas Aquinas teaches in the Summa Theologiae III, Q.64, A.6, Reply to Objection 2:9

'He who approaches a sacrament receives it from a minister of the Church, not because he is such and such a man, but because he is a minister of the Church. Consequently, as long as the latter is tolerated in the ministry, he that receives a sacrament from him does not communicate in his sin, but communicates with the Church from whom he has his ministry. But if the Church, by degrading, excommunicating, or suspending him, does not tolerate him in the ministry, he that receives a sacrament from him sins, because he communicates in his sin.'

And in the same treatise Saint Thomas states the conclusion in its own terms, Summa Theologiae III, Q.82, A.9:

'Heretical, schismatical, excommunicate, or even sinful priests, although they have the power to consecrate the Eucharist, yet they do not make a proper use of it; on the contrary, they sin by using it. But whoever communicates with another who is in sin, becomes a sharer in his sin. Consequently, it is not lawful to receive Communion from them, or to assist at their Mass. Still there is a difference among the above, because heretics, schismatics, and excommunicates, have been forbidden, by the Church's sentence, to perform the Eucharistic rite. And therefore whoever hears their Mass or receives the sacraments from them, commits sin.'

SSPX priests exercise ministry outside the Church's hierarchical communion, under the suspension established in Article 1:10 the 1976 suspension a divinis was formally declared and personally communicated, in a matter Pope Paul VI had explicitly framed in terms of the dimissorial letters requirement; Benedict XVI confirmed in 2009 that SSPX ministers do not legitimately exercise ministry in the Church. The 1988 episcopal consecrations were declared a schismatic act by Pope John Paul II in Ecclesia Dei adflicta; Cardinal Gantin's decree of excommunication, issued the previous day, cited Canon 1364, the canon for schism, alongside Canon 1387 (1382); and the DDF under Pope Leo XIV declared the excommunications of July 2, 2026 on the same ground. Priests ordained by bishops whose consecrations constitute schismatic acts, operating under declared suspension from a body the Holy See has refused canonical recognition in every pontificate since Paul VI, are not tolerated in the ministry. Therefore the faithful who knowingly receive from SSPX ministers communicate in their sin.

I answer that

The faithful sin by receiving Holy Communion from the hands of SSPX priests, provided they have sufficient knowledge of the canonical situation. Saint Thomas’s principle is clear: the key distinction is between a minister tolerated in his ministry and one the Church does not tolerate by virtue of suspension or excommunication.11 SSPX priests fall into the latter category — suspended by canonical act, declared by papal authority, and confirmed across every pontificate since Paul VI.

It must be acknowledged: no Vatican document explicitly declares 'receiving Communion at an SSPX Mass is a grave sin.' The present conclusion is derived from Aquinas's principle applied to the canonical situation established in Article 1, and it draws its strength from that premise, namely, the Church does not tolerate SSPX as public ministers. The question here is whether Rome's consistent position constitutes non-toleration in precisely Aquinas's sense. The answer is yes, for the following cumulative reasons: (1) Paul VI formally declared Lefebvre suspended a divinis in 1976 — a direct removal from ministry; (2) Benedict XVI explicitly stated that SSPX ministers 'do not legitimately exercise any ministry in the Church' even after lifting the excommunications in 2009; (3) the PCILT warned in 1996 that formal adherence to the Lefebvrian schism carries automatic excommunication. This warning addresses adherence, not mere attendance, but this presupposes the ministry “adhered to” is not tolerated by the Church; (4) Rome has refused to grant canonical status in every pontificate since Paul VI. This is not a contested judgment about a peripheral matter — it is the cumulative, consistent, formally expressed position of the Church over fifty years. The Church does not tolerate SSPX ministers in the ministry in precisely the sense Aquinas requires.

That said, the conclusion speaks objectively and does not accuse as mortally sinful every act of reception. Subjective culpability follows knowledge: the visitor who wanders into an SSPX chapel completely unaware of its status does not sin; others with confused or partial knowledge are guilty to the degree of their knowledge; those, however, who know the Church's judgment and knowingly receive are therefore guilty as we have stated above.

Reply to Objection 1. Validity Does Not Affect Reception from a Non-Tolerated Minister

See Objection

The objection correctly states the principle for priests in good canonical standing. However St. Thomas himself distinguishes the two cases in the same article.12 The priest in good standing celebrates on behalf of the Church — his personal sin is his own. The suspended priest acts on his own authority, having been removed from the Church's ministry. The valid confection of the sacrament and the lawfulness of receiving it are distinct questions. SSPX Masses are valid. The question is not sacramental validity but whether the faithful communicate in the minister's irregularity by knowingly receiving from him.

Reply to Objection 2. The Hawaii Five Ruling, Perl Letter, and PCILT Address Schism and Not the Thomistic Principle of Communication in the Sin of the Non-Tolerated Minister

See Objection

Cardinal Ratzinger's ruling13 addressed the canonical crime of schism under Canon 136414 — not the moral question of whether receiving Communion from a suspended minister involves the faithful in his sin under Aquinas's principle. Canonical crime and sinful actions are not identical categories. The Perl letter warrants the same distinction, and two further ones. First, a private response of the Pontifical Commission is not an authentic interpretation of law and carries no legislative force — a limit the Commission itself insisted upon when it publicly clarified in January 2003 that its September 2002 letter was a private communication addressed to one correspondent's circumstances. Second, the fulfillment of the Sunday obligation under Canon 1248 §1 turns on assistance at a Mass celebrated in a Catholic rite — a question distinct from whether knowingly receiving from a non-tolerated minister is licit, which is this Article's thesis. Indeed the same 2003 clarification stated that SSPX priests are suspended from exercising their priestly functions and that their Masses, though valid, are illicit — the very premise from which this Article argues. The 1996 PCILT clarification15 further distinguished occasional attendance without the schismatic spirit from habitual exclusive attendance combined with use of SSPX sacramental structures. The Hawaii Five ruling and the Perl letter are properly cited in SSPX-adjacent arguments, and their weight is acknowledged here. Neither, however, addresses the Thomistic principle at stake.

Reply to Objection 3. Probable Opinion Does Not Work When the Competent Authority Rules Otherwise

See Objection

Disagreement among theologians establishes a probable opinion — where the question remains open. But probabilism presupposes a doubt of law that authority has not resolved. Here the legislator himself has spoken on the meaning of his own discipline: Benedict XVI has stated that SSPX ministers do not legitimately exercise any ministry in the Church; the Holy See has refused canonical recognition in every pontificate since Paul VI; and the DDF's decree of July 2, 2026 reaffirmed the schismatic character of the Society's episcopal acts. A private theological opinion, however learned, cannot reopen a question against which the declared judgment of the lawgiver has closed. The theologians cited argue what the law should permit, or what supplied jurisdiction would cover if the conditions held; the competent authority to determine whether those conditions hold has consistently determined they do not. The question is not whether theologians disagree, but whether a probable opinion survives contrary resolution by the legislator. It does not.

Reply to Objection 4. Faculties for Confessions and Marriage Benefit the Faithful, Not Determine the Status of the Minister

See Objection

The Francis provisions supply jurisdiction for the faithful's benefit precisely because the faithful would otherwise be in an uncertain sacramental position. Rome's solution was to ensure validity for the faithful — not to declare SSPX ministry fully licit.16 The scope of the grants confirms this reading: Rome supplied faculties for precisely the two sacraments in which jurisdiction touches validity — penance and marriage — and granted nothing for the Eucharist, whose validity was never in question. The provisions repair what a defect of jurisdiction would invalidate; they leave the question of liceity exactly where it stood. A second reason is pastoral mercy: Rome does not want the faithful to lose access to sacramental grace due to clerical irregularity they did not create. This is analogous to the emergency provision of Canon 976, which permits even an excommunicated priest to absolve in danger of death — without that being a declaration that the excommunicated priest's ministry is otherwise licit. Finally, the objection’s premise no longer holds. The Explanatory Note of 2 July 2026 declares the sacrament of penance administered by the Society’s priests, and the marriages at which they assist, to be invalid (Appendix A, A.7, §3). It is no answer that the Note does not in terms revoke the faculties Pope Francis granted. A faculty is a concession of jurisdiction — exercisable only within the communion it presupposes, and revocable by the authority that grants it — not a possession the minister retains against the Church; and the same Canon 976 just cited shows that excommunication and valid absolution do not coexist outside danger of death. The competent authority has now declared these ministers schismatic and their penance and marriages invalid; that determination negates the very effect the earlier grant existed to produce, without needing to name it, and in the external forum the faithful are bound by it. What the provisions secured while the Society remained within reach of communion is answered above; what they secure now is nothing.

Reply to Objection 5. Canon 844 Does Not Apply to the SSPX Situation

See Objection

The objection conflates two distinct legal categories and stretches a narrow exception past its plain terms.

Canon 844 §1 states without qualification that Catholic ministers administer the sacraments licitly only to Catholics, who likewise receive them licitly only from Catholic ministers; §2 is a narrow, conditioned exception, not a general license. All conditions must hold simultaneously: necessity or genuine spiritual advantage; no danger of error or indifferentism; physical or moral impossibility of approaching a Catholic minister; and a minister belonging to a church whose sacraments are valid. The canon’s standard application — Catholics in Siberia or comparable regions with no resident Catholic clergy at all — shows ‘morally impossible’ means the practical absence of any Catholic minister, not the presence of one whose Mass is found liturgically unsatisfying. A licit Novus Ordo Mass, offered by a priest in good standing, is a Catholic minister in the canon’s sense. Its availability extinguishes the §2 exception on its own terms, regardless of one’s assessment of its liturgical merit. Reading ‘moral impossibility’ to include ‘access to a Mass I find spiritually deficient’ would empty the clause of content, since any dissatisfaction could be redescribed as a burden. The canon has never been interpreted by the Holy See to cover liturgical preference.

The objection trades on an equivocation latent in the word schismatic. Articles 2 and 3 use it in the sense Canon 751 defines — refusal of submission to the Roman Pontiff by a determinate act, and the personal canonical crime that formal adherence to such an act incurs under Canon 1364. Canon 844 uses ‘non-Catholic minister’ in a structurally different sense: a minister of a church Rome has institutionally recognized as existing in imperfect communion, per Unitatis Redintegratio 3,17 a determination Canon 844 §3 reserves explicitly to the Apostolic See’s own judgment. The Holy See has made that determination about the Orthodox; it has never made it about the SSPX. Indeed the opposite is true: that Rome subjects SSPX bishops to penalties under the Latin Code at all — most recently in the Decree of 2 July 2026, which grounded the excommunications in Canon 1364 §1 of the Code itself — presupposes they remain subject to that law as Catholics, since merely ecclesiastical law binds only the baptized Catholic (Canon 11).18 That is a jurisdiction Rome does not claim, and could not coherently claim, over Orthodox clergy. The schismatic character of an act, and the personal canonical crime of formal adherence to it, are categories internal to the Catholic Church’s own discipline. They do not convert the SSPX into a separate ecclesial body of the kind Canon 844 references.

The objection stated that if you can receive from the Orthodox, then a fortiori you can receive from the SSPX. But this inference runs backward. The objection argues the SSPX case is easier because its priests affirm the papacy while Orthodox clergy deny it. But Canon 844 §2 does not turn on the minister’s doctrinal soundness — it turns on the petitioner’s access to any Catholic minister at all. The Orthodox exception is not a reward for correct doctrine; it is a narrow accommodation to physical isolation. SSPX doctrinal fidelity, however genuine, is not the variable the canon regulates.

The operative distinction in this Article remains Aquinas’s tolerated and non-tolerated minister, not Canon 844. The canon answers whether a Catholic may receive from a minister outside the Church; this Article asks whether a Catholic may receive from a suspended minister inside the Church. It is precisely the second question the objection fails to answer.

Reply to Objection 6. Canon 1335 §2 Answers Hidden Censures — the SSPX's Censure Is Public and Declared

See Objection

This is the strongest objection in the Article, yet it still fails to demonstrate it is morally licit to receive Communion from the SSPX. And this for three reasons:

First, the condition the canon protects — a censure hidden from the faithful who approach the minister — is absent here. Paul VI declared the suspension a divinis in 1976; John Paul II declared the excommunications and the schismatic character of the 1988 consecrations in Ecclesia Dei adflicta; the DDF declared the excommunications of July 2, 2026. Benedict XVI stated in 2009 that the Society's ministers do not legitimately exercise any ministry in the Church. Canon 1335 §2 covers the private censure unknown to the public — the priest under a hidden impediment. Consider a classic example: a priest who violated the seal of confession in a private conversation and incurs latae sententiae excommunication under Canon 1386 §1 (former Canon 1388 §1, on the seal). Suppose immediately after this conversation, he has to celebrate Sunday Mass for his parish. Due to the hidden nature of his censure, Canon 1335 §2 would apply. Nothing exterior would alert the faithful to his canonical status. The SSPX priest's status is not private in this sense. His impediment is the public, declared, and repeatedly reaffirmed position of the Apostolic See toward the body whose ministry he exercises. To apply a provision designed for hidden censures to a ministry whose irregularity has been proclaimed by six popes is to read the letter against the mind of the legislator (Canon 17). Nor does the strict reading of the July 2026 Decree change this. Grant for the sake of argument everything the objection draws from Canon 18: that the Decree declares censures for six bishops alone, and that its conditional warning to clerics and faithful concedes the excommunication of Canon 1364 has not yet fallen on them. All of this touches on the question of the excommunication — a question this Article's argument does not need to win. The argument rests on the suspension established in Article 1 and on the non-toleration of the Society's ministry, declared in 1976 and restated in 2009 — a standing condition the 2026 Decree presupposes rather than creates. Read as strictly as the objection wishes, the Decree leaves the Aquinas premise exactly where it has stood for fifty years; and its Nota Esplicativa, far from retreating, states the ministers' condition in the present tense: non-tolerated.

Second, the canon's own rationale confines it to a concrete act, not a standing arrangement. A provision protecting the faithful's access to sacraments in the moment of request cannot justify a permanent parallel sacramental system. The scale the "request" reaches is no longer one of the faithful approaching a minister, but the faithful collectively sustaining the very ministry the censure exists to suppress — the exception swallowing the rule it qualifies. The just cause the canon recognizes in the individual request does not become permission for the habitual scheduling of “requests” on a chapel calendar, any more than Canon 976's emergency absolution licenses an excommunicated priest to open a confessional.19 And where reception becomes habitual and structural, a distinct consideration enters: the 1996 PCILT Note — which addresses not this canon but the conditions of adherence — warned that making the Society's structures one's stable provision shades into the formal adherence that carries its own canonical consequence. The objection is correct: the Note is not an authentic interpretation under Canon 16 §1, and nothing here rests on its legislative force. It is invoked for what it articulates, not what it enacts: the distinction between occasional attendance and formal adherence flows from Canons 751 and 1364 themselves, and the Note's statement of it has now been expressly adopted by the DDF's Nota Esplicativa of July 2, 2026, which declares the Note "still in force" and makes its conditions the Dicastery's own.20 The Note may lack the force of law and yet remain the authoritative statement of how the legislator understands his own law. This is precisely the standing this Article's argument requires of it, and no more.

Third, and decisively, the canon suspends the minister's prohibition; it does not settle the faithful's cooperation. Canon 1335 §2 answers whether the censured priest violates his censure by ministering when asked — a question about his juridical position, enacted for the faithful's benefit. It does not touch the prior moral question of what the requester does. Nor does the suspension of the prohibition legitimate the ministry: the canon removes the minister’s delict in the act of ministering when asked; it confers neither faculties nor mission, and the exercise remains illegitimate though unpunished. Requesting a sacrament from a non-tolerated minister is, at minimum, material cooperation in the illegitimate exercise of his ministry. Where the reception is habitual and the Society's chapel becomes one's stable provision, the cooperation becomes proximate, sustains the irregular ministry itself, and adds scandal. When joined to adherence to the Society's position against the Apostolic See, it becomes formal, and the faithful communicate in the minister's sin in exactly the sense Aquinas describes.

  11. Excommunication: The most severe censure in the Church's penal system, forbidding the excommunicated person from celebrating or receiving any sacrament, exercising any ecclesiastical ministry, or participating in acts of governance (Canon 1331). Unlike suspension, excommunication also forbids reception of the sacraments. Excommunication does not remove a person from the Church but places them in a state of separation from the Church's communion. It is medicinal in intent, aimed at bringing the person to repentance.
