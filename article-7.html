Article 7

Article 7: Whether Arguments From Ecclesial Inconsistency, From the Crisis in the Church, and From the Sincerity and Suffering of the Faithful Bear Upon the Liceity of Receiving Holy Communion From the SSPX.

Objection 1. The Anglican Comparison: Fraternal Prayer with Canterbury vs. Excommunication of Traditional Priests

The severity of the sanctions is difficult to reconcile with Rome’s conduct toward those genuinely outside the Church. On 27 April 2026, some nine weeks before the Society was excommunicated, Pope Leo XIV received and prayed with Sarah Mullally, the first woman to hold the See of Canterbury1 — a minister of a communion the Catholic Church holds to lack valid orders altogether (Apostolicae Curae; Dominus Iesus). If the Roman Pontiff extends fraternal prayer to one who ordains women and blesses same-sex unions, while excommunicating priests whose offense is fidelity to the Church’s perennial faith and liturgy, the sanction cannot be an even-handed act of justice; it betrays an animus against Tradition rather than a concern for unity.

Objection 2. The Synodal Way Comparison: Selective Discipline and Unpunished Dissent

Furthermore, the same disparity appears within the Church. The German “Synodal Way” has advanced positions on sexual morality, the priesthood, and Church governance in open tension with settled teaching, and Fiducia Supplicans (2023) authorized blessings for couples in irregular unions;2 yet no comparable canonical sanction has fallen on their proponents. When doctrinal dissent at the Church’s center is met with accompaniment, and only traditional Catholics at its margins are met with excommunication, the faithful reasonably conclude that the discipline is selective, and so not binding in conscience as an act of justice.

Objection 3. The Pachamama Objection: The Moral Authority of the Sanctioning Prelates

Furthermore, the moral authority of those imposing the penalty is itself in question. The faithful recall the veneration of the “Pachamama” images during the 2019 Amazon Synod3 and ask, in the phrase now common among them, “excommunicated by whom?” A censure issued by prelates who have themselves tolerated what the faithful regard as idolatry or grave doctrinal confusion cannot bind the conscience of one who keeps the whole of the Catholic faith.

Objection 4. "Instructed and Confirmed": The Society's Plea and the Maxim That an Unjust Law Is No Law

Furthermore, the Society asked, in its own words, to be “instructed and confirmed in the faith of all time,” and was “declared schismatic a second time.”4 It sought bread and was given a stone. A penalty that falls upon men for their fidelity, and that the Society itself judges “objectively unjust and invalid,” cannot oblige the faithful, for an unjust law is no law.

Objection 5. Four Decades of Negotiation: Schism as a Label Rome Manufactured

Furthermore, the charge of schism has never truly held. Rome negotiated with the Society for four decades, Pope Benedict XVI remitted the 1988 excommunications in 2009, and Pope Francis granted its priests faculties for confession and marriage. A body with which the Apostolic See prays, negotiates, and shares faculties is not a separated sect; the renewed schism label is a rupture Rome has manufactured, not one the Society created.

Objection 6. Necessary to Preserve the Faith in an Unprecedented Crisis

Furthermore, the Church herself is in a crisis without precedent, and the Society exists to preserve the traditional faith through it. Its Profession of Catholic Faith of 24 June 2026 — one hundred fifty-four paragraphs addressed to Leo XIV and annotated throughout from the Church's own magisterium — professes the whole of Catholic doctrine,5 and a bishop in full communion has publicly affirmed that the Society does ‘nothing else as to teach and proclaim the same faith as did the saints until the council.’6 Its Superior General teaches that one belongs to the Church above all through the integral profession of the faith, and that no one can be made to choose between the Faith and the Church.7 If modernist error has penetrated even the Church's highest levels, then to assist at the Society's Masses is not to leave the Church but to remain with her tradition: the faithful who kneel at its altars are not communicating in schism but resisting error, and preserving the faith of their baptism where alone it is preached without compromise.

Objection 7. Without the Consecrations, We Would Lose the Traditional Latin Mass

Finally, without the Society's episcopal consecrations the Traditional Latin Mass would have perished, and the faithful would have been left with only the Novus Ordo Missae. It was Archbishop Lefebvre who kept the ancient liturgy alive when it had all but vanished from the Church's altars, and it is the Society's continued existence that restrains Rome from suppressing it altogether. The faithful who assist at the Society's Masses are therefore not communicating in disobedience but safeguarding a treasure of the Church; had they waited upon permissions, there would be nothing left to permit.

On the contrary, (The Donatist Principle: Validity Does Not Depend on the Worthiness of the Minister)

The lawfulness of an individual’s reception of a sacrament is determined by the objective character of the minister’s act and by his communion with the Church, not by the consistency, the motives, or the personal holiness of those who govern. Saint Augustine settled the principle against the Donatists: the validity and the effects of the Church’s acts do not depend upon the worthiness of the minister.8 And the Council of Constance condemned the proposition of Jan Hus that a prelate in mortal sin does not truly govern — fixing for jurisdiction the same principle Augustine had fixed for the sacraments.9

I answer that

These arguments have real rhetorical force, and honesty requires conceding the truth in them: the Church's discipline is not applied with perfect consistency, and that unevenness is a genuine wound and a scandal to many sincere souls. But not one of them reaches the question the faithful actually face. The first four are either an argument that others sin worse unpunished (the tu quoque), or an argument that the men who govern are unworthy to govern (the Donatist error), or an appeal to the sincerity and suffering of those sanctioned (a question of imputability, not of liceity). The fifth mistakes forbearance for toleration, and mistakes provisions since revoked for a standing recognition of ministry. The sixth confuses the bond of faith with the bonds of communion, as though material orthodoxy could supply for hierarchical submission. The seventh argues from consequences, as though a good preserved could make licit the means of preserving it. None of them touches the single point on which reception turns: whether the minister is one the Church tolerates in his ministry (Article 5). The inconsistency of a physician does not make a poison a medicine.

Reply to Objection 1. Two Registers: Ecumenism Toward the Separated, Governance Toward the Suspended

The comparison is a category error — the very one dismantled in Article 5, Reply to Objection 5. Prayer with the Archbishop of Canterbury belongs to the Church’s ecumenical dealings with a separated communion, whose members the Holy See does not treat as Catholics subject to its discipline (Canon 11); the Pope prayed with Mullally precisely as with one outside full communion. The Society’s bishops, by contrast, are Catholics whom Rome disciplines under its own Code. To receive fraternally one who is outside, and to discipline one who is inside, is not a double standard but the difference between two registers — ecumenism and internal governance. The Anglican comparison, far from helping the objection, confirms the distinction on which this work rests: the Society is not a separated church, but suspended Catholic clergy.

Reply to Objection 2. The tu quoque Fallacy: Others' Unpunished Sin Confers No License

The unevenness is real, and the grievance is not baseless; that dissent at the Church’s center often escapes sanction is a scandal this work does not minimize. But “others sin worse and are not punished” has never, in Catholic moral theology, made one’s own act licit. The tu quoque is a fallacy in logic and, dressed in pastoral clothing, remains one in morals: Rome’s failure to discipline the Synodal Way confers on no one a right to communicate in the act of a suspended minister. If anything the argument proves too much, for it would permit any crime by pointing to a worse one left unpunished.

Reply to Objection 3. Donatism Applied to Governance

That the worthiness of the one imposing a penalty does not determine its binding force is not a matter of opinion but of settled doctrine. To hold that a censure binds only if issued by the holy is the Donatist error carried into governance — and on governance the Church has spoken as plainly as on the sacraments, condemning at Constance the proposition that a prelate in mortal sin does not truly rule. The efficacy of the Church's juridical and sacramental acts does not depend on the sanctity of the minister. Whatever the force of the "Pachamama" complaint as a lament over the state of the Church, as an argument against the binding force of a canonical penalty it is the error of Wyclif and Hus applied anew, and it fails as it failed at Constance.

Reply to Objection 4. Imputability Is Not Liceity

The sincerity of the Society and the real suffering of its faithful are not in doubt, and they matter — but they bear on imputability, which Article 1 has already treated, not on liceity. A subjectively sincere act can be objectively illicit; a penalty can bind even where the one who incurs it believes himself faithful. That the Society judges the sanction "unjust and invalid" is its own assertion, answered in Article 4; no man is judge in his own cause, and the Church's penal decrees enjoy the presumption of validity until the competent authority, upon lawful recourse, sets them aside (Canon 124 §2). Even an unjust but not null censure is to be feared until it is lawfully annulled (Article 4, Reply to Objection 2).

Reply to Objection 5. Forbearance Is Not the Absence of the Delict

A father who pleads for decades with a departing son does not thereby prove the son has not left. Rome's long patience, the remission of 2009, and the grant of faculties show forbearance and pastoral mercy, not the absence of the delict. The faculties themselves were framed as extraordinary provisions granted for the pastoral benefit of the faithful, not as a recognition of licit ministry;10 and the acts of 2 July 2026 have declared that “the sacrament of penance administered by them and the marriage assisted by them are invalid,”11 so that the datum on which the objection chiefly rests no longer exists. Indeed the Decree of 2 July 2026 carries the very protocol number of the 2009 remission, marking that act of mercy as one the renewed consecrations have now undone. The Society's separation is not a label Rome invented in 2026; it is the canonical situation the Society created in 1988, was offered the means to heal in the Protocol it first signed and then abandoned, and has now renewed at Écône.

Reply to Objection 6. Orthodoxy Alone Does Not Excuse Schism

The crisis is real, and this work has not denied it; nor is the Profession's orthodoxy in question across the great body of its paragraphs — its doctrine of God, of Christ, of Our Lady, of the sacraments and the last things is the doctrine of Trent and of Vatican I, cited from the Church's own sources. Indeed the document is itself evidence for the central contention of this work: no separated confession composes a profession of faith annotated from Denzinger and addresses it to the Roman Pontiff. Men who write to the Pope as their father are Catholics under his discipline, not ministers of another church (Article 5).

But schism, in the Church's own definition, is not heterodoxy; it is "the refusal of submission to the Supreme Pontiff or of communion with the members of the Church subject to him" (Canon 751). A man may hold every article of the Creed and still refuse that submission, and the Profession documents the refusal with the same candor with which it documents the orthodoxy. It rejects the Council's teaching on religious liberty in itself, and not merely its abuses; it negates, nearly word for word, the Council's teaching on the imperfect communion of separated Christians and on the rays of truth in the non-Christian religions; it rejects the received doctrine on the episcopal college and the legitimacy of the liturgical reform.12 And beneath these particular rejections lies the formal one: the Profession reserves to the professing subject the judgment of which acts of the living Magisterium bind, measuring the present teaching office against the past as the Society reads it. This is to displace the proximate rule of faith from the living Magisterium, where the Church has fixed it, to a private adjudication of the prior Magisterium — the very hermeneutic of rupture Benedict XVI identified in 2005, which is shared, as he observed, by those who welcome the rupture and by those who deplore it.13 The Profession professes that it judges no one and usurps no authority; its own articles do the judging.14

Nor is the Superior General's teaching — that one belongs to the Church above all by the profession of the integral faith — the traditional doctrine it claims to be. Pius XII teaches that those really belong to the Church who are baptized, profess the true faith, and have neither separated themselves from the unity of the Body nor been excluded by legitimate authority:15 the bond of faith is one bond of three, and the faith itself includes what the Church has always taught about her own visible, hierarchical communion (Article 2). To invoke the integral faith against the requirement of communion is therefore not to escape the dilemma but to profess the faith minus one of its articles.

Even were everything the objection says of the crisis granted, the conclusion would not follow, for orthodoxy confers no faculties. Suspension falls upon the orthodox and the heterodox alike; the question on which reception turns — whether the Church tolerates the minister in his ministry (Article 5) — is not altered by the soundness of the minister's preaching. The faithful who would resist error must resist it as Catholics have always resisted it: within the Church's communion, not at altars she has forbidden. To preserve the traditional faith by means the traditional faith condemns is not preservation; it is the crisis, entered rather than resisted.

Reply to Objection 7. The Traditional Latin Mass Is Preserved by Fidelity and Unity, Not Disobedience

Archbishop Lefebvre's movement was a real factor in the survival of the Traditional Latin Mass. But the objection rests on a counterfactual asserted as fact, and counterfactuals cut both ways: we cannot know what would have happened without the Society — nor how the ancient rite would have flourished under bishops with full pontifical mandate, had the Protocol of 1988 been upheld.16 What we do know is what did happen. The rite was never abolished, and permission for it, restricted but real, has never lapsed: the English indult of 1971, the indult of 1984, the Ecclesia Dei adflicta communities of 1988, the declaration of 2007 that the old Missal was never abrogated, and even the restrictions of 2021, which curtailed but did not suppress — while in 2022 the Holy See expressly confirmed the Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter in its proper liturgical books.17 Extinction has been prophesied at every turn; at every turn it has remained a prophecy.

Indeed the objection nearly inverts the causality. When Rome did restrict the ancient rite, the stated reason was not the rite itself but its use as a banner of division and of rejection of the Council. That association — the Traditional Mass with disobedience — is the Society's most enduring contribution to the question,18 and the communities in full communion have carried it as a burden ever since, obliged to persuade their bishops that love of the ancient liturgy is not a badge of schism.19 If the Traditional Mass has enemies, nothing has armed them so well as its defense by illicit means. Obedience within the hierarchy is harder than independence. Operating in the Church requires patience, negotiation, and submission that autonomy avoids entirely. The easier path does not make it the correct one any more than the difficulty of the straight and narrow prevents it from being the way to Heaven.

And even if the Society's disobedience did win concessions, it is never lawful to do evil that good may come (Romans 3:8); that God draws good from evil is the Creator's prerogative, not the creature's license. The deeper reason lies in the thing defended. The Eucharist is, in Augustine's words, the sign of unity and the bond of charity;20 a sacrament of unity is not preserved by rupturing unity — that is to save the sign by destroying what it signifies. Israel received the Temple and the Land and lost both, not because God's gifts fail, but through infidelity to the Giver; the prophet's warning fell precisely on those who cried "the temple of the Lord, the temple of the Lord" while refusing obedience (Jeremiah 7:4). If the ancient liturgy is pleasing to God — and the Church herself calls it venerable21 — it will not be lost except as the Temple was lost. It is kept as every gift of God is kept: by fidelity and obedience within the household of the faith.
