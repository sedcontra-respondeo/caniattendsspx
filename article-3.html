Article 3

Whether the Episcopal Consecrations Performed by the Society of Saint Pius X on July 1, 2026 Likewise Constituted a Schismatic Act.

Objection 1. Institutional Extinction Creates Genuine Necessity

The 2026 consecrations differ materially from those of 1988. In 1988, the SSPX had been operating for only eighteen years and had no episcopal successors. In 2026, after thirty-eight years, the SSPX faces genuine institutional extinction: its three bishops were reduced to two by the death of Bishop Bernard Tissier de Mallerais in October 2024 (a fourth, Bishop Richard Williamson, expelled from the Society in 2012, died in 2025), both remaining bishops are in their late sixties, and no mechanism exists for the continuation of priestly ordinations without new bishops. This is a more acute necessity than in 1988, and constitutes the kind of emergency for which the principle of necessity was designed. Bishop Athanasius Schneider, Auxiliary Bishop of Astana, formally appealed to Pope Leo XIV on February 24, 2026, asking him to grant the apostolic mandate for the consecrations, arguing that the Holy See should ‘build a bridge to the SSPX, this portion of his flock, and permit the episcopal consecrations on an exceptional basis in order to foster a climate in which, through greater mutual trust, a solution to the doctrinal questions and the corresponding juridical arrangements may be found patiently and gradually.’ That a bishop in full communion found the succession crisis sufficiently urgent to appeal directly to the Pope speaks to the genuine institutional gravity of the situation.1 2

Objection 2. Openness to Dialogue Shows No Refusal of Submission

Furthermore, the SSPX approached Rome prior to the 2026 consecrations and expressed willingness for doctrinal dialogue. Fr. Pagliarani's letter of February 18, 2026 welcomed renewed discussions while declining to postpone the consecrations.3 This demonstrates that the SSPX is not refusing submission to the Pope in principle — it is proceeding under necessity while remaining open to resolution. This is not the posture of a schismatic body but of one seeking regularization through the only means available to it given the urgency of its situation. Bishop Schneider reinforced this assessment on EWTN in May 2026, noting that the SSPX's Declaration of Catholic Faith is ‘entirely Catholic’ and ‘written with such devotion to the Holy Father’ — conduct inconsistent with schismatic intent.4 Schneider also observed that SSPX priests name the reigning Pope in the Canon of the Mass and name the local bishop, acts of canonical acknowledgment incompatible with the posture of a body that has severed communion.

Objection 3. Thirty-Eight Years of Unresolved Theological Dispute

Furthermore, the canonical and theological arguments against the 1988 consecrations have been contested for thirty-eight years by serious theologians and canonists. The SSPX's canonical study, the Gleize articles in Courrier de Rome, and multiple canonical analyses argue that the consecrations do not constitute schism properly understood. Given this genuine theological dispute, the faithful of the SSPX cannot be said to be in formal schism by adhering to their community. Bishop Schneider’s document The Core Question Regarding the Priestly Society of Saint Pius X (June 4, 2026) argues that the controversy ‘extends beyond questions of ecclesiastical discipline and reflects doctrinal and liturgical disputes that have persisted in the Catholic Church since the Second Vatican Council.’ He further observes that the SSPX’s unconditional acceptance of Vatican II is presented as a condition for full communion while no comparable doctrinal requirement has been imposed on communities with far more radical departures from tradition. A bishop in full communion thus holds that the theological dispute is genuine, unresolved, and insufficiently engaged by those pressing the canonical charge of schism.5 6

Objection 4. Rome's Double Standard in China and Ukraine

Furthermore, Rome's application of the schism charge to the SSPX is rendered canonically and morally incoherent by its own inconsistent enforcement. In 2025, two episcopal consecrations were performed within the Chinese Catholic Church without prior papal mandate, in direct violation of Canon 1387 (1382) and of the 2018 Sino-Vatican agreement itself — and Leo XIV subsequently accepted and regularized these bishops rather than declaring their consecrations schismatic acts or imposing excommunication.7 Cardinal Josyf Slipyj, Major Archbishop of the Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church, likewise consecrated bishops without explicit papal mandate in 1977, and Rome never declared these consecrations schismatic or applied any excommunication.8 If unauthorized episcopal consecrations performed by Chinese Communist Party-appointed bishops, or by a cardinal acting under political duress, do not constitute schismatic acts warranting excommunication, then the same canonical logic must apply to the SSPX, which consecrates bishops for confessedly orthodox purposes, in explicit acknowledgment of papal legitimacy, and without any claim to territorial jurisdiction. The selective application of Canon 1387 (1382) and the schism charge — rigorously enforced against the SSPX while accommodated or retroactively regularized in cases involving political convenience — reveals that what is at issue is not canonical principle but institutional politics. A law that is applied to one party and not another in materially identical circumstances ceases to function as law and becomes an instrument of discrimination. This double standard deprives Rome's schism declaration of the moral authority and canonical consistency it requires to bind in conscience.9

Objection 5. The Crisis in the Church Is Worse Than in 1988

Furthermore, even if the necessity invoked in 1988 were judged insufficient, the condition of the Church has not improved in the intervening thirty-eight years but deteriorated. Fr. Pagliarani, asked directly in February 2026 whether the need of souls is as serious today as in 1988, answered: "I think it's even more evident today... I think that today it is even worse. Much worse. The decisions made by Pope Francis are catastrophic. Yes, catastrophic," adding that in 1988 "we had not yet reached this point," whereas "today, everything is collapsing."10 Commentators sympathetic to the Society have developed the same argument at length: the diagnosis of a systemic crisis comes not from private judgment but from the teaching Church herself across several pontificates, and "the state of necessity is today even greater than in 1988."11 The continuity of traditional doctrine and liturgy is now more at risk under Traditionis Custodes and a neo-Modernist hierarchy than when Archbishop Lefebvre acted.12 Whatever necessity sufficed to justify the consecrations in 1988, a worse crisis supplies greater necessity now.

On the contrary, (The 2026 Consecrations Are Structurally Identical to 1988 and Carry the Same Consequences)

The 2026 consecrations are structurally identical to those of 1988 in every canonical and theological respect that matters. Canon 1387 (1382) applies without modification.13 The Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith, under Cardinal Fernández and with the approval of Leo XIV, issued a formal warning on May 13, 2026 declaring the planned consecrations 'a schismatic act' carrying automatic excommunication.14 Leo XIV personally requested the SSPX reconsider. Fr. Pagliarani publicly announced the consecrations on February 2, 2026.15 On February 12, the Holy See, with papal approval, offered structured theological dialogue on condition that the consecrations be suspended; on February 18 the Society welcomed the dialogue and refused the condition.16 The structure of refusal is identical to 1988: a legitimate means of resolution was offered and declined.

I answer that

The 2026 consecrations constitute a schismatic act for the same reasons as the 1988 consecrations, with the additional aggravating circumstance that thirty-eight years of consistent Roman teaching, multiple offers of regularization, and the personal intervention of a new Pope have all been set aside.

These consecrations have since taken place. On June 29, 2026 — the Solemnity of Saints Peter and Paul — Leo XIV personally wrote to Fr. Pagliarani, pleading with the Society to turn back and describing, in his own voice, ‘the schismatic act you are about to undertake.’17 The Society proceeded nonetheless. On July 1, 2026, at Écône,18 Bishop Alfonso de Galarreta, assisted by Bishop Bernard Fellay,19 consecrated four new bishops without pontifical mandate. On July 2, the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith declared that the consecrating and the consecrated bishops had ipso facto incurred the latae sententiae excommunication reserved to the Apostolic See under Canons 1387 and 1364 §1, that the Society’s sacred ministers are in schism, and that the faithful who formally adhere to the Society are likewise excommunicated.20 The Pope’s letter, the Decree, and the Explanatory Note are reproduced in full in Appendix A; Fr. Pagliarani’s reply, the consecration homily, the declaration read in place of the apostolic mandate, and the General House statement are cited there with links to the published originals. What Article 2 established of the act of 1988, the events of July 1–2, 2026 confirm of its repetition: the same absence of mandate, the same explicit and personal papal prohibition, and the same declaration of a schismatic act by the competent authority in real time.

Reply to Objection 1. Necessity Fails When the Offered Means Was Refused

The succession crisis is a real institutional problem, but it does not constitute the kind of necessity that can justify episcopal consecration against the explicit papal prohibition. Necessity, as established in Reply to Objection 2 of Article 2, requires that no legitimate means be available. Saint Thomas likewise defines the necessary as that which ‘cannot be otherwise’ (Summa Theologiae I-II, Q.93, A.4); yet the situation could indeed have been resolved otherwise, within a regularized canonical structure. Rome offered structured theological dialogue, with papal approval, directed at resolving the SSPX's canonical situation — the framework within which episcopal succession could lawfully have been sought and provided.21 This offer was refused. Nor was the mandate itself beyond asking: Bishop Schneider's formal appeal to Pope Leo XIV on February 24, 2026, petitioning for the apostolic mandate, shows that the lawful path lay open to the very day of the act. Necessity cannot be invoked when the necessary means were available and rejected. Furthermore, the succession crisis — while genuinely urgent from the SSPX's institutional perspective — is in part a consequence of the SSPX's own thirty-eight years of refusal to normalize its canonical situation, during which multiple paths to regularization were offered and declined. The Society has since confirmed, in its own words on the day itself, that this is the ground on which it acted: Fr. Pagliarani’s consecration homily calls the act ‘exceptional measures, proportionate to this necessity,’ and grounds it in the principle ‘that the supreme law is the salvation of souls,’ which, he says, God ‘applies to the letter.’22 This restates the plea already answered; it does not repair it. Necessity is established not by the gravity of the want but by the want of any lawful means to meet it, and a lawful means was offered and refused. Nor does raising the appeal to the level of a divine law strengthen it: the salvation of souls is the supreme law of the Church as administered through her constituted authority, not against it.

Reply to Objection 2. Proceeding Anyway Is Not Offset by Talk of Dialogue

Proceeding with a prohibited act while expressing willingness for future dialogue does not constitute submission to papal authority — it constitutes its inverse. The Pope's requirement was that the consecrations be suspended as a condition of dialogue. To proceed with the consecrations while expressing openness to talking about them afterward is to act against the express condition the Pope set. This is not the posture of obedience with necessity; it is the posture of disobedience with good manners. The Bisig essay's observation from 1988 applies with equal force here: 'To pretend after all that to be still united to the Church and even to the Pope means either to recognize for the Vicar of Christ a primacy of honor, as do the avowed schismatics, or to commit a blundering incoherence.'23 The homily supplies the theology beneath this posture. Fr. Pagliarani denies that any choice between the Faith and the Church can arise: ‘We belong to the Church first of all through the Faith, through the integral profession of the Faith … just as we belong to a family because we bear the same name.’ (Appendix A, A.3) On this account one remains in the Church by right profession alone, and canonical communion forms no part of membership. This answers a question the Church has not asked. No one charges the Society with heresy; the charge is the refusal of submission to the Roman Pontiff by a determinate act (Canon 751), and a theology of membership-by-faith cannot dissolve a delict defined by disobedience rather than belief. It must be said plainly, lest the present argument be mistaken for the Society’s own: this work does not adopt the premise that juridical communion is irrelevant to membership. It holds the contrary. The very proof that the Society’s clergy remain Catholics under the Church’s discipline is that Rome subjects them to the penalties of her own Code (Canon 11; Article 5, Reply to Objection 5).

Reply to Objection 3. Ongoing Dispute Does Not Make the Charge False

Theological and canonical dispute about the consecrations does not dissolve the schismatic character of the act. The existence of SSPX canonical studies and sympathetic academic commentary does not change what Pope John Paul II declared, what Pope Benedict XVI maintained even while remitting the personal censures — that the Society has no canonical status and its ministers exercise no legitimate ministry — and what Leo XIV has now reiterated. The Bisig essay noted in 1988 that the SSPX's canonical defense failed to cite 'one single recognized theologian or Father of the Church, or a single pontifical or conciliar document' acknowledging the legitimacy of episcopal consecration against the Pope's express will — and thirty-eight years of effort have not produced such a citation.24 Debate does not create equal probability on both sides when the weight of the tradition, the consistent papal position, and the divine-constitution argument from Pius XII and Vatican I all point in one direction. As the Bisig/de Blignières essay concludes: 'The Society has rejected the obvious solution because it appears in contradiction with the analysis done...This solution consists on [sic] confidence in the promises of Christ to his Church: visibility, petrinity, indefectibility, and formal apostolicity.'25 The Society’s own July documents confirm this rather than unsettle it. Neither the homily nor the Superior General’s reply of 30 June engages the canonical charge on its merits: neither mentions Canon 1387 or Canon 1364, nor the warning that the act would be schismatic, but answers throughout at the level of spiritual self-justification — which declines the charge rather than rebutting it.26 And the Society continues to read 1988 as vindication, not error. Fr. Pagliarani asks the Pope to find in the mere continuation of dialogue ‘proof that the Society is neither schismatic nor hostile to the Church,’ (Appendix A, A.2) and in the homily he holds up Archbishop Lefebvre — ‘not yet canonised’ — as the model the new bishops must follow, adding that ‘thirty-eight years ago, they condemned a saint.’ (Appendix A, A.3) A body that treats the condemned act of 1988 as the pattern for 2026 has not resolved the dispute in its favor; it has chosen to repeat it. That serious men dispute the charge does not make it false, and the Society’s own witness shows the dispute is not over what was done, but over whether the Apostolic See had authority to forbid it.

Reply to Objection 4. Inconsistent Enforcement Does Not Void the Canon

The double-standard argument has rhetorical force and genuine pastoral sting, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that Rome's inconsistent enforcement of Canon 1387 (1382) creates real credibility problems. Nevertheless the argument fails on three grounds.

First, the cases are not canonically parallel, and not for the reason sometimes claimed. Cardinal Slipyj was not physically prevented from reaching Rome — he had lived there since 1963, fourteen years before the 1977 consecrations. The real disanalogies are three. Rome never called it schism: no decree ever characterized Slipyj's act as schismatic, and no excommunication was ever declared or applied — a fact SSPX's own commentators have called puzzling, but which is undisputed. The manner was opposite: Slipyj acted secretly, precisely to avoid a refusal he anticipated; the SSPX acted at a five-hour public ceremony before some 15,000 people, in explicit, publicized defiance of Leo XIV's personal, written request — days before the fact — that they reconsider. The emergency was categorically different: the Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church was then illegal, underground, and facing the death of its persecuted hierarchy, with no signed accord, no structural offer, and no functioning channel of communication with Rome — every one of which the SSPX had, and set aside. A quiet act of succession-planning by an elderly confessor of an outlawed Church, never called schism and never punished, is not a precedent for a public, defiant act that Rome declared schismatic within twenty-four hours.27

Second, the Chinese case is more complex than the objection presents, but it actually strengthens rather than weakens the contrary position. The 2018 Sino-Vatican agreement was itself an attempt to bring Chinese episcopal consecrations within a framework of at least partial papal involvement — an acknowledgment by Rome that the unauthorized consecrations were problematic and required regularization, not a declaration that they were canonically equivalent to authorized ones. When Leo XIV subsequently accepted bishops consecrated in violation of that agreement, he did so in the context of a live diplomatic process aimed at eventual full regularization — not as a declaration that Canon 1387 (1382) does not apply or that the consecrations were unobjectionable. More pointedly: the Chinese bishops operate under a Communist state that does not recognize the Pope's authority over internal Chinese affairs — they act within a system of coercion that, whatever its moral failures, is not a free choice to defy papal authority. The SSPX, operating freely in Switzerland and multiple democratic countries, with full access to Rome, with multiple offers of regularization on the table, faces no comparable external constraint. The comparison flatters the SSPX by conflating coerced irregularity with freely chosen defiance.28

Third, and most importantly, the argument proves too much. If inconsistent enforcement of a canon by ecclesiastical authority dissolved the canon's binding force, then every canonical penalty would be voidable simply by identifying another case where the same penalty was not imposed — a principle that would dissolve canonical order entirely. The Church's failure to apply Canon 1387 (1382) consistently in every case does not change what the canon requires or what the act objectively is. It may give rise to legitimate criticism of Rome's diplomatic priorities, and that criticism is not without weight. But canonical inconsistency is a failure of enforcement, not a determination of canonical truth. The SSPX's act is what it is regardless of what Rome has done or not done in Beijing. The double standard, if real, is Rome's problem to answer for — it is not a license for the SSPX to proceed as though the canon does not exist.29

Reply to Objection 5. Necessity Admits No Degrees; the Necessity Has Lessened Since 1988, Not Grown; and the Society Cannot Be Judge of Its Own Necessity

Although we might grant the premise for the sake of argument, the conclusion still does not follow. First, the objection mistakes the structure of the principle it invokes. Necessity, as used in the objection, becomes a sliding scale: a graver crisis licenses a graver remedy. However no such scale exists. Article 2 has established that the requirement of the pontifical mandate belongs to the divine constitution of the Church, which necessity cannot override (Article 2, Replies to Objections 2 and 4), and the Pontifical Council for the Interpretation of Legislative Texts articulated the consequence exactly: a state of necessity "must be verified objectively," and "there is never a necessity to ordain Bishops contrary to the will of the Roman Pontiff."30 Never is not a proportion. An argument that the crisis has worsened adds weight to a scale the Church teaches does not exist; it cannot make licit in 2026 what was excluded in principle in 1988, any more than a greater anger licenses the murder of one’s enemies.

Second, the objection conflates the general crisis of the Church with the particular necessity the consecrations purport to remedy — the continuation of traditional priestly formation and sacramental life — and on the particular point the facts run opposite to the objection's premise. In 1988 that provision scarcely existed outside Écône; in 2026 it exists worldwide, for the Holy See herself, in the wake of 1988, provided for it through the Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter, the Institute of Christ the King, and the Institute of the Good Shepherd. As Fr. Gerald Murray observes, consecrating bishops against the will of the Pope is a necessary means of providing new priests "only if it plans to continue operating apart from the Holy See."31 Nor is this a partisan observation: the Society's own defenders concede that this line of thinking is common precisely among traditionalists and SSPX sympathizers who reject the 2026 consecrations.32 The Society itself does not deny the fact: at the same roundtable, its own moderator granted that "today, undoubtedly more than in 1988," numerous communities throughout the world benefit from the Traditional Mass, and the Superior General's answer was not that such provision does not exist but that it lacks "guarantees" — that these communities live under a "Sword of Damocles" of episcopal discretion.33 But this quietly replaces the necessity pleaded with a different one: no longer the want of the sacraments and of traditional formation, which is conceded to be supplied, but the want of a guarantee of permanent independence from the judgment of the hierarchy. That is not a necessity the Church could ever supply without ceasing to govern; it is a demand for the very autonomy whose assertion constitutes the schismatic character of the act. Whatever may be said of doctrinal confusion in general, the specific want the consecrations claim to answer has lessened since 1988, not grown — and what remains of it is the Society's own choice.

Third, the objection cannot answer the question its own advocates have posed: who declares the state of necessity?34 If the gravity of the crisis is the measure, the measurement rests with the party seeking the exemption — and the worse the Society judges the crisis, the more completely it has substituted its own judgment for the Church's. Carried to its term, the argument proves that the Church has failed in her ordinary mission and must be rescued by an extraordinary one — the very conclusion Pius XII condemned when the identical plea of necessity was made for the Chinese consecrations,35 and the one the founders of the Fraternity of Saint Peter refused in 1988 by placing their confidence instead in the promises of Christ to His Church, as Reply to Objection 3 has already recalled. A necessity whose existence, gravity, and remedy are all certified by the party it exempts is not necessity but sovereignty.

Fourth, whatever ambiguity attended 1988, none attended 2026. The consecrations followed despite a personal plea of Leo XIV, who explicitly named "the schismatic act you are about to undertake" (Appendix A, A.1). A worsened crisis might in some other matter bear on imputability; it cannot convert an act performed after explicit papal prohibition into anything but deliberate choice. Necessity describes the absence of alternatives; contumacy describes their refusal.

Canonical principle that inconsistent enforcement does not void a canon's force: The principle that a law's binding force is not dissolved by inconsistent enforcement is standard in both civil and canonical jurisprudence. In canon law specifically, the medicinal character of penalties (Canon 1312) means that pastoral prudence in enforcement is not only permitted but encouraged — the decision not to impose a penalty in a given case reflects pastoral judgment about the offender's disposition and the likelihood of amendment, not a determination that the law does not apply. Canon 1341's requirement that ordinaries exhaust pastoral means before imposing penalties explicitly contemplates uneven enforcement as a feature rather than a defect of the canonical system.↩︎
